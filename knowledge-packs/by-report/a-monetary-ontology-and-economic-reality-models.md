# **A. Monetary Ontology & Economic Reality Models — The Nature of Money, Credit, Collateral, and Financial Abstraction**

## **Introduction: The Central Tension and the Illusion of Simplicity**

To the casual observer, money appears to be a simple, homogeneous object.1 It is used daily with minimal cognitive friction to buy goods, settle debts, and measure value.3 In mainstream economic textbooks, this simplicity is codified through the classic three-function definition: money is a medium of exchange, a unit of account, and a store of value. However, this functional definition conflates the retail experience of money with its wholesale operational reality.

In the actual functioning of modern financial systems, money is not a single neutral commodity, but a dynamic, highly structured hierarchy of layered promises.1 Every form of money is someone else’s balance-sheet liability, and its operational behavior changes fundamentally during periods of economic contraction or systemic stress.1

This hierarchy is constructed on a basic dualism between "money"—the asset accepted for final settlement of an obligation—and "credit"—a promise to pay money in the future, which delays final settlement.1 The boundary between these two categories is not fixed; what functions as money at one level of the financial system represents mere credit to the level above it.1

For example, a retail customer views a commercial bank deposit as the ultimate means of payment.3 In reality, that deposit is merely an unsecured demand promise issued by a private bank.3 When that customer uses a debit card to buy groceries, the transaction is settled instantly at the retail level.3

At the wholesale level, however, the transaction is not complete.8 The customer’s bank must transfer an equivalent value of its own assets—specifically central bank reserves—to the grocer’s bank.8 At this wholesale clearing level, bank deposits are treated as credit instruments, and only central bank reserves function as money.1

This structural complexity becomes visible when the system is subjected to stress.5 During periods of economic expansion, credit expands, the hierarchy flattens, and the quantitative differences between different forms of credit and final money are ignored.5

In a crisis, the hierarchy steepens.5 Market participants experience a collective shift in liquidity preference, refusing to accept subordinated credit promises and demanding settlement in the highest-quality, most liquid liabilities available.5 This behavior highlights the "survival constraint": the daily operational necessity for every financial actor to manage cash inflows and outflows to maintain convertibility and prevent insolvency.1

### **The Commodity vs. Credit/State Tensions**

The transition from a commodity-based monetary standard to a pure fiat standard has kept the debate between metallist and chartalist traditions active.12 Metallists argue that money must be a commodity with an exchange value independent of its form as currency.12 Under this view, fiat currencies are accepted only because of a historical path dependency tracing back to a metallic standard.13

Chartalists counter that the value of money is established when the state defines the unit of account and declares what tokens will be accepted at its pay-offices to settle tax liabilities.12 Modern chartalists stress that the state does not need to collect taxes before it can spend; rather, it must spend its currency into existence before the public can acquire the tokens necessary to pay taxes.12

### **The Exogenous vs. Endogenous Money and Loanable Funds Debate**

The debate between exogenous and endogenous money represents a key fault line in banking theory. The exogenous monetarist tradition relies on the "loanable funds" model, which posits that savers deposit physical cash in banks, and banks subsequently lend those funds to borrowers.7 This framework suggests that the central bank can limit bank lending by restricting the quantity of reserves it provides.7

In contrast, the endogenous credit-money tradition demonstrates that commercial bank lending is not constrained by reserves.7 Whenever a bank approves a loan, it creates a new deposit liability in the borrower’s account, expanding the broad money supply.7 In modern central banking, reserves are supplied on demand to clear interbank transactions and stabilize overnight rates.7

### **Reserves as Settlement Assets vs. Public Spending Money**

A conceptual error in some heterodox schools is the treatment of central bank reserves as a form of money that can be directly spent into the real economy.8 Reserves are wholesale settlement assets restricted to commercial banks and authorized financial institutions holding accounts at the central bank.8

Reserves cannot be lent directly to retail customers because those customers do not have access to reserve accounts.8 When the government spends, it draws on its treasury account at the central bank, which shifts reserves to commercial bank clearing accounts, while the commercial banks credit the deposit accounts of the public.12 Thus, reserves serve to clear wholesale interbank obligations, while commercial bank deposits function as the primary currency for the real economy.8

### **Monetarist Quantity Targets vs. Interest-Rate Operating Systems**

The monetarist assumption that central banks can control inflation by targeting the growth rate of a specific monetary aggregate (e.g., M2) has been largely abandoned in practice.7 Modern central banks operate via interest-rate targeting.7

In an "ample reserves" or "floor" system, the central bank maintains an abundant supply of reserves, setting the Interest on Reserve Balances (IORB) rate as the primary tool to anchor short-term interbank interest rates.20 Rather than trying to control the quantity of money, central banks adjust the price of central bank money to influence credit demand and lending standards throughout the monetary hierarchy.1

### **Printing vs. Balance-Sheet Expansion**

The term "money printing" is often used to describe central bank asset purchases, but this description is operationally inaccurate.7 When a central bank buys assets through Quantitative Easing (QE), it expands its balance sheet by purchasing securities from non-bank financial institutions.7

This transaction creates central bank reserves for the intermediary commercial bank and a corresponding deposit liability for the selling financial institution.7 It is a balance sheet swap that exchanges a long-term sovereign bond for a highly liquid, short-term deposit asset.7 This process does not alter the net wealth of the private sector, but it does change the maturity profile and liquidity of its assets.7

### **Liquidity vs. Solvency**

A clear analytical distinction must be maintained between liquidity challenges and solvency challenges.6 A liquidity crisis occurs when a solvent institution has assets that exceed its liabilities but is temporarily unable to convert those assets into cash to meet immediate cash-flow obligations.6

A solvency crisis occurs when the fundamental market value of an institution's assets falls below its liabilities, making it unable to meet its long-term obligations even if given access to emergency funding.6 Central banks can address liquidity crises by acting as a lender or dealer of last resort, but they cannot resolve a structural solvency crisis without fiscal intervention or asset restructurings.1

### **Dollar Dominance vs. De-Dollarization**

Despite ongoing discussions around de-dollarization and the rise of alternative settlement networks, the U.S. dollar maintains its position at the apex of the global monetary hierarchy.1 This dominance is not solely due to the size of the domestic U.S. economy, but is sustained by the depth and liquidity of U.S. capital markets, the global offshore Eurodollar market, and the role of the Federal Reserve as a global provider of dollar liquidity through swap lines.9

While central banks have diversified their reserve portfolios into non-traditional currencies, the offshore dollar system remains the primary infrastructure for international trade invoicing, debt issuance, and wholesale capital flows.20

### **Stablecoins vs. Institutional Settlement**

The rise of privately issued digital stablecoins has introduced a new element to the monetary hierarchy.22 While stablecoins are marketed as digital alternatives to traditional cash, they are functionally private credit instruments backed by short-term reserves and high-quality liquid assets.22

They do not settle in central bank money.10 Instead, they settle on public or private distributed ledgers, relying on commercial bank deposits or reserve assets for final conversion.22 This setup subjects them to run risks similar to historic shadow banking instruments if the quality or convertibility of their backing assets is questioned.22

## **The Inherent Hierarchy: Liquidity, Convertibility, and Balance Sheet Physics**

The monetary system is hierarchical, meaning that what functions as money at one layer of the system represents credit to the layer above it.1 This structure can be visualized as a symmetric pyramid built on a credit-to-money axis.

                  /\\   
                 /  \\  (Ultimate Money)   
                /----\\  
               /      \\  
              /        \\ (Money to Commercial Banks; Credit to Central Bank)   
             /----------\\  
            /            \\  
           /              \\ (Money to Retail Public; Credit to Banks) \[5, 7, 9\]  
          /----------------\\  
         /                  \\  
        /                    \\ (Credit Instruments / Shadow Monies) \[4, 22, 23, 25\]  
       /----------------------\\  
      /                        \\ \[Lines of Credit / Unsecured Promises\]  
     /                          \\ (Pure Credit Promises / Off-Balance Sheet) \[5, 33\]  
    /----------------------------\\

The position of any asset within this hierarchy is determined by its liquidity and convertibility—specifically, the ease and speed with which it can be converted into the ultimate asset of final settlement without a loss of value.1 Under this framework:

* **Sovereign Reserves & Gold**: Form the ultimate anchor of the international payment system.4 They represent outside money, meaning they are assets to their holder and are not backed by a corresponding private sector liability.6  
* **Central Bank Reserves & Currency**: Represent the ultimate settlement assets within a domestic jurisdiction.1 They are the liabilities of the state or its central bank.5  
* **Commercial Bank Deposits**: Function as inside money, representing private sector assets matched by commercial bank liabilities.6 Under normal conditions, they are convertible on demand into central bank currency or reserves at par.3  
* **Securities, Repos, and Shadow Monies**: Represent highly liquid private or sovereign credit promises.4 They are not means of payment for retail transactions but serve as cash-equivalent funding and collateral instruments within wholesale financial markets.23  
* **Lines of Credit**: Represent contingent, off-balance sheet credit promises that can generate active deposit liabilities upon draw.9

This hierarchical structure is dynamic and shifts across the economic cycle.5 During periods of economic expansion, credit is easily accessible, and the distinction between credit and final money becomes attenuated.5

In this expansion phase, the hierarchy flattens.5 Market participants accept a wide range of private credit promises (such as commercial paper, repo claims, and asset-backed securities) as cash equivalents, driving down liquidity premiums and interest rate spreads.1

When a crisis occurs, this process reverses, and the hierarchy steepens.5 Private credit promises are no longer accepted at face value, and market participants seek to convert their holdings into safer, more liquid assets further up the pyramid.5

This dynamic causes a steepening of the monetary pyramid.5 Private credit contracts, liquidity spreads widen, and the demand for ultimate settlement assets (central bank reserves and sovereign Treasury bills) increases.5

To trace these dynamics, one must examine the relationships between the balance sheets of the central bank, the commercial banking system, and the private sector.5

Central Bank Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\-------------------------------------+-------------------------------------  
Sovereign Securities / Gold  | Central Bank Reserves (Bank Assets)   
Loans to Banks (Discounts) | Currency in Circulation

\-------------------------------------+-------------------------------------  
Central Bank Reserves  | Private Sector Deposits (Money)

Private Sector (Non-Bank) Balance Sheet

\-------------------------------------+-------------------------------------

Capital Goods / Real Estate | Net Worth (Equity) 

Within this balance sheet network, market participants manage liquidity and convertibility through their transactions.1 In normal times, a securities dealer acts as a market maker, standing ready to buy or sell securities in exchange for bank deposits.6

Similarly, a commercial bank acts as a specialized dealer, standing ready to convert its deposit liabilities into cash or central bank reserves at a fixed 1-to-1 ratio.3 The central bank sits at the apex, standing ready to maintain convertibility between national currencies and international settlement assets.6

When private market makers face liquidity constraints and cannot clear transactions, the central bank must step in as a dealer of last resort to stabilize the monetary hierarchy and prevent a systemic collapse.1

## **Operational Mechanics of Money and Credit Creation**

Understanding how money and credit are created and destroyed requires analyzing the double-entry balance sheets of the commercial banking system and the private sector.8 This operational analysis dispels the classic "money multiplier" and "loanable funds" theories, showing instead how the credit extension process actually functions.7

### **Endogenous Credit Creation (The Loan Initiation Phase)**

When a commercial bank approves a loan for a borrower, it does not transfer physical cash or pre-existing central bank reserves to that borrower.8 Instead, the bank creates a deposit of the corresponding size on its own balance sheet.8

At that moment, new broad money (inside money) is created.7 The bank's assets expand by the value of the new loan (the borrower's promise to pay), and its liabilities expand by the value of the new deposit (the bank's promise to pay on demand).7

Commercial Bank Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\+ $500,000 (Loan Asset: Borrower's IOU) | \+ $500,000 (Deposit Liability: Bank's IOU) 

Borrower's Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\+ $500,000 (Deposit Asset: Bank's IOU) | \+ $500,000 (Loan Liability: Borrower's IOU) 

At the moment of credit creation, no change in central bank reserves occurs.8 The bank's capacity to create this loan is constrained by its capital adequacy ratios, risk tolerance, and the availability of creditworthy borrowers, not by its reserve holdings.7

### **Interbank Clearing and Reserve Settlement**

Although a bank can create deposits through lending, it faces a liquidity constraint when the borrower spends those deposits.1 If the borrower uses the funds to purchase an asset from a seller who banks at a different institution, the borrower’s bank must transfer the deposits, which are cleared at the wholesale level using central bank reserves.8

Bank A Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\- $500,000 (Reserves at Central Bank) | \- $500,000 (Borrower's Deposit Liability)

Bank B Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\+ $500,000 (Reserves at Central Bank) | \+ $500,000 (Seller's Deposit Liability)

In this transaction, the receiving bank (Bank B) gains reserves, while the transferring bank (Bank A) loses them.8 For the banking system as a whole, the total volume of central bank reserves remains unchanged.8

If Bank A faces a temporary reserve shortfall, it must borrow reserves from other banks in the interbank market, access central bank lending facilities, or sell assets to rebuild its reserve position.8

### **The Destruction of Credit**

Just as lending creates money, the repayment of bank loans destroys money.7 When a borrower repays the principal of a loan using their deposits, the transaction reduces both the assets and the liabilities on the bank’s balance sheet, contracting the aggregate broad money supply.8

Commercial Bank Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\- $500,000 (Loan Asset)  | \- $500,000 (Deposit Liability) 

### **Quantitative Easing (QE) as an Asset Swap**

To stimulate the economy during low-interest-rate environments, central banks use Quantitative Easing (QE).7 Unlike endogenous credit creation, which is driven by private credit demand, QE is a balance sheet expansion initiated directly by the central bank.7

When the central bank purchases a sovereign bond from a non-bank financial institution (such as a pension fund), it must clear the transaction through the pension fund's commercial bank, as non-banks do not hold reserve accounts.8

Central Bank Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\+ $1,000,000 (Sovereign Securities)  | \+ $1,000,000 (Reserves owed to Bank A) 

Commercial Bank (Bank A) Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\+ $1,000,000 (Reserves at Central Bank) | \+ $1,000,000 (Pension Fund Deposits) 

Pension Fund Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
\- $1,000,000 (Sovereign Securities) | No Change  
\+ $1,000,000 (Deposits at Bank A) |

This transaction increases both the volume of reserves in the banking system and the volume of commercial bank deposits held by the public.7 The reserves created are locked within the central bank’s settlement system and cannot be directly lent to households or non-bank businesses.8

The primary transmission mechanism of QE is not direct lending, but the portfolio rebalancing effect: the pension fund, now holding low-yielding deposits, reinvests these funds in higher-yielding corporate bonds or equities, which drives up asset prices and lowers borrowing costs for firms.7

## **The Global Sovereign and Shadow Architecture: Eurodollars and FX Swaps**

The global role of the U.S. dollar is supported by an offshore financial network: the Eurodollar system.20 This offshore dollar market operates outside the direct regulatory authority of the U.S. government, providing international wholesale funding.20

### **Structure and Function of the Eurodollar Market**

Eurodollars are unsecured U.S. dollar-denominated deposit liabilities booked at bank offices located outside the United States.20 Because these banks operate offshore, they are exempt from domestic U.S. banking regulations, Federal Reserve reserve requirements, and FDIC deposit insurance assessments.20 This regulatory exemption allows offshore banks to operate with narrower interest rate margins, attracting global capital.29

Offshore Bank (Eurobank) Balance Sheet  
Assets | Liabilities  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
Loans to Foreign Corporations (USD) | Eurodollar Time Deposits (USD)   
Clearing Balances at US Banks (USD) | Interbank Eurodollar Borrowing 

To clear and settle payments, Eurobanks do not maintain reserves at the Federal Reserve; instead, they hold dollar clearing balances at onshore U.S. correspondent banks.29 Thus, nearly all offshore Eurodollar transfers are ultimately resolved via book-entry adjustments on the ledgers of onshore U.S. financial institutions.34

### **Foreign Bank Organizations (FBOs) and Reserve Arbitrage**

Within these wholesale markets, Foreign Bank Organizations (FBOs) operating through U.S. branches and agencies perform a highly specialized role.20 Most FBO branches in the United States are uninsured by the FDIC, which prevents them from accepting domestic retail deposits.20

Consequently, they are heavily dependent on short-term wholesale unsecured funding, representing over 90% of the daily volume in Eurodollar and selected deposit markets, and between 65% and 95% of the federal funds market.20

This institutional setup facilitates a reserve arbitrage trade.20 Federal Home Loan Banks (FHLBs)—which are Government-Sponsored Enterprises—are the dominant lenders of federal funds, accounting for over 90% of daily volume.20 FHLBs maintain cash accounts at the Federal Reserve but are legally prohibited from earning Interest on Reserve Balances (IORB).20 They are therefore incentivized to lend their excess cash overnight at a rate below the IORB.20

Because FBO branches are uninsured and exempt from paying the FDIC deposit insurance assessment fee (unlike insured domestic banks), they face a lower effective cost of borrowing.20 FBO branches borrow funds from the FHLBs in the federal funds market at rates below the IORB and place them directly into their reserve accounts at the Federal Reserve to earn the higher IORB rate, capturing a risk-free spread.20

### **Regulatory Arbitrage and the Transition to Onshore Selected Deposits**

The offshore dollar system has been reshaped by regulatory changes since the Great Financial Crisis:

* **Dodd-Frank Resolution Plans ("Living Wills")**: Mandated that global banks simplify their corporate structures to facilitate orderly resolution in a crisis.20 This prompted FBOs to wind down offshore Caribbean booking entities, moving their Eurodollar borrowing onshore to U.S. branches.20  
* **Repeal of Regulation Q (2011)**: Eliminated the historical prohibition on banks paying interest on demand deposits, facilitating the creation of economically equivalent onshore "selected deposits".20  
* **Elimination of Reserve Requirements (2020)**: In March 2020, the Federal Reserve reduced reserve requirement ratios to zero percent.20 Prior to this, selected deposits required reserve holdings, while Eurodollar deposits did not.20 The elimination of this reserve requirement removed the final cost differential, prompting selected deposits to overtake Eurodollars, accounting for 85% of the combined overnight volume by 2022\.20

### **FX Swaps and Central Bank Liquidity Swap Lines**

When offshore financial institutions require U.S. dollar funding but cannot access unsecured wholesale markets, they utilize the foreign exchange (FX) swap market—the largest source of global dollar liquidity, with a daily turnover exceeding $3.8 trillion.31

An FX swap consists of two legs.9 At initiation, a foreign bank sells its domestic currency (e.g., Japanese Yen) to a counterparty in exchange for U.S. dollars at the spot exchange rate, while simultaneously entering into a forward agreement to buy back its domestic currency at a future date at a pre-specified exchange rate, eliminating foreign exchange risk.9

Under Covered Interest Parity (CIP), the forward premium should offset the interest rate differential between the two currencies.17 However, under market stress, balance sheet constraints prevent arbitrageurs from intermediate funding, leading to CIP violations (where offshore borrowing costs for USD through FX swaps rise above onshore rates).31

To alleviate these funding pressures, the Federal Reserve utilizes a network of central bank liquidity swap lines.9

Federal Reserve Balance Sheet | Bank of Japan Balance Sheet  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
Assets: \+ JPY Reserves (at BoJ) \[9\] | Assets: \+ USD Reserves (at Fed) \[9\]  
Liabilities: \+ USD Reserves (owed to BoJ) | Liabilities: \+ JPY Reserves (owed to Fed) \[9\]

Bank of Japan Balance Sheet | Commercial Bank Balance Sheet  
\------------------------------------------+------------------------------------------  
Assets: \- USD Reserves (at Fed) | Assets: \+ USD Reserves (at Fed)  
Assets: \+ Domestic Collateral (from Bank) | Liabilities: \+ JPY Collateral / IOU to BoJ

At initiation, the Fed exchanges U.S. dollars for the foreign central bank's currency at the spot rate, with an agreement to reverse the transaction at the same rate in the future.9 The foreign central bank then distributes these dollars to local commercial banks via auctions.9 The foreign central bank bears the credit risk of the local loans and guarantees the return of dollars to the Fed, shielding the Fed from counterparty credit risk.30

Swap lines alleviate offshore funding costs through two distinct channels:

1. **The Substitution Channel**: Non-U.S. banks obtain dollars directly from their central bank, reducing their demand for dollars in the private FX swap market, thereby compressing CIP deviations.31  
2. **The Lending Channel**: Non-U.S. banks that borrow dollars through the swap line supply these dollars to the FX swap market to capture the premium on U.S. dollar lending or to satisfy precautionary hoarding requirements, increasing the global supply of offshore dollars.31

## **Collateral Physics, Rehypothecation, and Market Plumbing**

In wholesale financial markets, credit is rarely extended on an unsecured basis.23 Modern financial plumbing is powered by collateral.21

### **Collateral as High-Powered Money**

Just as the traditional banking system creates broad money through the deposit-multiplier process, the shadow banking system creates credit and liquidity through the reuse and velocity of pledged financial collateral.23 In this context, marketable collateral functions as a form of high-powered money.23

The primary suppliers of collateral to the market (the "source" collateral pool) are non-banks, such as hedge funds executing leveraged strategies, and custodians holding securities on behalf of pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds, and official sector accounts.23 Large global banks and dealer-brokers act as intermediaries, operating central collateral desks that receive this pledged collateral and reuse it to meet the funding and collateral needs of other market participants.23

### **Collateral Velocity and Re-use Rates**

The capacity of the financial system to intermediate credit is dependent on collateral velocity—the average number of times a single piece of source collateral is reused or repledged across the financial system.21

Analogous to the money multiplier, where the reserve ratio determines the theoretical limit of deposit expansion, the haircut (the percentage reduction applied to the market value of collateral to shield lenders from price volatility) acts as the reserve ratio in the collateral chain.23 The length of the collateral chain represents the multiplier.23

Theoretical Collateral Multiplier \= 1 / Haircut

The aggregate volume of collateral-backed credit is determined by:

Aggregate Secured Credit \= Source Collateral \* Collateral Velocity 23

Under normal market conditions, collateral velocity typically ranges between 2.5 and 3.0.27 However, this velocity is highly sensitive to counterparty risk.39

### **Regulatory Plumbing: US Rule 15c3-3 vs. United Kingdom Regulations**

The reuse of pledged collateral is governed by distinct regulatory frameworks across jurisdictions, creating opportunities for regulatory arbitrage:

* **United States**: The practice of rehypothecation is strictly capped.39 Under SEC Rule 15c3-3 and Regulation T, a broker-dealer is prohibited from using its customer's securities to finance its proprietary activities and can rehypothecate an amount only up to 140% of the customer's debit balance.39  
* **United Kingdom**: Prime brokers operate under a different legal framework.39 There are no customer protection rules capping collateral reuse; an unlimited amount of the customer's assets can be rehypothecated.39

This regulatory divergence prompts global hedge funds to negotiate Prime Brokerage Agreements in London rather than New York, allowing prime brokers to reuse their collateral in exchange for lower financing fees.39

### **The Quantitative Easing Collateral Drain**

A structural conflict exists between unconventional monetary policy (Quantitative Easing) and collateral market efficiency.21 When a central bank engages in QE, it purchases massive volumes of high-quality liquid assets (specifically sovereign debt, such as U.S. Treasuries) and places them on its own balance sheet.27

Once these securities enter the central bank's portfolio, they are effectively siloed and taken out of circulation.27 They cannot be rehypothecated or reused by the private shadow banking system, resulting in a collateral velocity of zero.27

By substituting highly versatile, reusable sovereign collateral with central bank reserves (which can only be held by banks and have a velocity of zero in the shadow financial system), QE reduces the pool of active, tradeable collateral.16 This can create a severe collateral scarcity, driving repo rates down (sometimes into negative territory), widening collateral wedges, and reducing the overall efficiency of financial plumbing.21

### **Liquidity and Margin Spirals**

The vulnerability of collateralized markets to sudden, non-linear contractions is modeled by Markus Brunnermeier and Lasse Heje Pedersen through the interaction of market liquidity (the ease with which an asset can be traded near its fundamental value) and funding liquidity (the ease with which traders can obtain funding/capital).11

Because traders must finance the difference between the purchase price of a security and its collateral value using their own capital, their trading capacity is bounded by their available equity and the margin (haircut) charged by financiers.40

Total Margin Commitment \= Sum over i of (Position Size\_i \* Haircut\_i) \<= Speculator Capital 40

When funding conditions tighten or a negative asset price shock occurs, two reinforcing feedback loops—the margin spiral and the loss spiral—can trigger a systemic liquidity crisis 40:

* **The Loss Spiral**: A negative shock reduces speculators' capital, forcing them to liquidate assets.40 This selling pressure drives asset prices down, inflicting further losses on their remaining portfolios, which depletes their capital even more and triggers further forced liquidations.40  
* **The Margin Spiral**: When speculators reduce their positions, market liquidity declines, causing price volatility to rise.40 Because financiers are uncertain whether price movements are driven by fundamental news or liquidity shocks, they raise margin requirements and haircuts to protect themselves.40 This increase in haircuts further restricts speculators' funding capacity, forcing them to deleverage and sell more assets, which exacerbates market illiquidity.40

These liquidity spirals explain why market liquidity can suddenly dry up, leading to a rapid transition from a high-liquidity, low-margin equilibrium to a low-liquidity, high-margin crisis state.11

## **Regulatory Governance: Solvency, Liquidity, and the Basel III Constraint Matrix**

Following the Great Financial Crisis, international banking regulators introduced the Basel III framework.18 These rules rely on three complementary, non-risk-weighted metrics designed to limit excessive leverage and address funding and maturity mismatches.33

### **The Leverage Ratio**

The Leverage Ratio acts as a non-risk-weighted capital backstop.33 Under risk-weighted assets (RWA) frameworks, banks can hold massive volumes of sovereign bonds or low-risk mortgages with very little capital backing because their risk weights are near zero.33 The leverage ratio acts as a floor, ensuring that a bank holds a minimum level of loss-absorbing capital against its total on-balance-sheet and off-balance-sheet exposures, regardless of perceived risk.33

Leverage Ratio \= Tier 1 Capital / Total Leverage Exposure \>= 3.0% 33

(Note: Under U.S. regulations, the minimum requirement is 5.0% for large systemically important bank holding companies and 6.0% for their insured depository institutions).43

Because the denominator includes the gross, non-netted value of securities financing transactions (repos and securities lending), the leverage ratio heavily penalizes low-margin, high-volume repo market making.26 This reduces the balance sheet space that global banks are willing to allocate to intermediating collateral, increasing market volatility during periods of stress.26

### **The Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)**

The LCR is a short-term, 30-day survival test designed to ensure banks maintain an adequate stock of unencumbered High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) that can be easily converted into cash to meet net cash outflows during an acute stress scenario.33

LCR \= Stock of HQLA / Total Net Cash Outflows over 30 days \>= 100% 33

HQLA is categorized into three tiers, reflecting differing liquidity profiles:

* **Level 1 Assets**: Central bank reserves and sovereign bonds (eligible for inclusion with a 0% haircut and no cap).33  
* **Level 2A Assets**: High-quality covered bonds and sovereign debt with a lower credit profile (subject to a 15% haircut and capped at 40% of total HQLA).33  
* **Level 2B Assets**: Blue-chip equities and lower-rated corporate bonds (subject to a 50% haircut and capped at 15% of total HQLA).33

Net cash outflows are calculated by applying standardized run-off rates to the bank's liabilities (e.g., higher run-off rates for unsecured wholesale funding than for stable retail deposits).33

Crucially, borrowing from Federal Home Loan Banks (FHLBs) carries a lower LCR run-off factor than borrowing from non-depository financial institutions or money market funds in the Eurodollar market.20 Consequently, borrowing in the federal funds market (dominated by FHLB lending) is highly advantageous from an LCR perspective, prompting banks to pay a premium (higher interest rates) for federal funds relative to Eurodollars during periods of balance sheet optimization.20

### **The Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)**

While the LCR addresses short-term shocks, the NSFR targets structural funding mismatches over a one-year horizon, encouraging banks to fund long-term assets with long-term, stable liabilities.33

NSFR \= Available Stable Funding (ASF) / Required Stable Funding (RSF) \>= 100% 33

* **Available Stable Funding (ASF)**: The portion of capital and liabilities expected to be reliable over a one-year horizon, calculated by multiplying the carrying value of liabilities by regulatory weights (e.g., regulatory capital and long-term debt receive a 100% ASF weight, while unstable wholesale funding receives 0%).42  
* **Required Stable Funding (RSF)**: A function of the liquidity characteristics and residual maturities of the bank's assets.42 Highly liquid Level 1 assets require little or no stable funding (0% to 5% RSF weight), whereas long-term unrated loans and non-performing assets require 100% stable funding.44

### **Interaction and Substitutability of Ratios**

These regulatory ratios do not operate in isolation; they interact dynamically to shape credit growth and bank behavior.18 During periods of economic stability, these constraints are non-binding for well-capitalized institutions.18

However, during financial stress, a relationship of partial substitutability emerged between the leverage ratio, the LCR, and the NSFR.18 This interaction can result in a "robust-yet-fragile" banking topology: while individual banks appear resilient according to risk indicators, the overlapping constraints reduce their collective capacity to intermediate credit and liquidity, increasing the risk of systemic bottlenecks under a significant shock.18

## **Global Reserve Dynamics, Dollar Dominance, and Settlement Evolution**

The international monetary system remains structurally dollar-centric, though the tracking of official reserves and the plumbing of wholesale settlement are undergoing significant operational changes.1

### **De-Dollarization Trends and IMF COFER Revisions**

According to the International Monetary Fund's Currency Composition of Official Foreign Exchange Reserves (COFER) survey, global exchange reserves totaled $13.14 trillion in the fourth quarter of 2025\.32 The U.S. dollar maintains its position at the apex of the reserve hierarchy, though its share has experienced a gradual, long-term decline.32

| Reserve Currency | Q4 2025 Share (%) | Q3 2025 Share (%) | Q1 2025 Share (%) | Structural Trend |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **U.S. Dollar (USD)** | 56.77% | 56.92% | 57.74% | Gradual decline; remains globally dominant.32 |
| **Euro (EUR)** | 20.25% | 20.33% | 20.06% | Stable, secondary reserve asset.32 |
| **Chinese Renminbi (RMB)** | 1.95% | 1.93% | 2.12% | Flat to declining; limited by capital account controls.32 |
| **Identified Others (JPY, GBP, AUD, CAD, CHF)** | 14.90% | 15.18% | 20.10% (Combined) | Gradual diversification into liquid G10 currencies.32 |
| **Unidentified Other Currencies** | 6.13% | 5.61% | Not separately tracked | Rapid, non-linear growth; doubling since 2021\.32 |

The decline in the USD share is not primarily a shift toward major competitors like the Euro or Renminbi, but represents a diversification into smaller, non-traditional currencies.32

To improve the analytical utility of this data, the IMF implemented a significant methodological change in Q3 2025, completely eliminating the "unallocated" portion of the COFER series.46 Previously, reserves of non-reporting countries or discrepancies were grouped as unallocated.48

The IMF now uses staff imputation procedures—specifically carry-forward, stratified mean, and mixed-method donor-based imputations—to provide a complete currency composition covering 100% of global foreign exchange reserves.46

### **Evolution of Wholesale Settlement: Distributed Ledger Technology and wCBDC**

Wholesale transaction settlement has historically relied on central bank real-time gross settlement (RTGS) platforms, such as the Eurosystem’s TARGET Services (T2/T2S).10 These platforms reduce credit and liquidity risk by ensuring that transactions settle in central bank money, the safest and most liquid settlement asset.10

However, the increasing interest in tokenized assets and Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) is driving central banks to adapt their infrastructure.10 If financial institutions shift toward DLT-based platforms for securities trading (delivery versus payment, or DvP) or cross-currency settlements (payment versus payment, or PvP), central banks must provide corresponding settlement assets on these ledgers.10

In response, the Eurosystem and other major central banks have initiated experiments to bridge legacy RTGS systems with private DLT platforms or to issue native wholesale Central Bank Digital Currencies (wCBDC).10 These solutions aim to ensure that wholesale transaction settlement continues to occur in central bank money, preserving the role of public money at the apex of the payment hierarchy and preventing the fragmentation of global liquidity.10

## **Macro-KB Ontology Registry**

To establish a consistent, operational vocabulary for the Macro-Financial Systems knowledge base, the following registry defines the core terminology and balance sheet roles of the monetary architecture.

### **Inside Money**

* **Definition**: Money created within the private financial sector, representing a balance sheet liability of a private institution matched by a private sector asset.6  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Commercial Bank Deposits (Liability of bank; Asset of private sector).5  
* **Systemic Function**: Serves as the primary circulating medium of exchange in the real economy.9  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Subject to run risk and steepening of the hierarchy.5

### **Outside Money**

* **Definition**: Money created outside the private financial sector, representing a liability of the sovereign state or central bank, backed by no corresponding private sector liability.6  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Central Bank Reserves and Cash (Liability of Central Bank; Asset of banks/public).5  
* **Systemic Function**: Functions as the ultimate means of final settlement and system-wide anchor of trust.1  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Absolute safety; experiences strong inflows.10

### **High-Powered Money (Monetary Base)**

* **Definition**: The primary monetary liabilities of the sovereign issuer, consisting of central bank reserves and physical currency in circulation.6  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Liabilities of the Central Bank.5  
* **Systemic Function**: Serves as the wholesale settlement asset and structural foundation for broad money creation.6  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Highly inelastic; velocity of circulation may collapse.6

### **Broad Money**

* **Definition**: The total stock of liquid assets held by households and non-bank companies, primarily consisting of bank deposits and circulating currency.7  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Assets of the Private Non-Bank Sector; Liabilities of Banks.5  
* **Systemic Function**: Determines the volume of transactional liquidity available in the real economy.7  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Contracts during deleveraging cycles.7

### **HQLA (High-Quality Liquid Assets)**

* **Definition**: Assets that can be converted into cash easily and immediately in private markets with little or no loss of value.33  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Assets of Commercial Banks.33  
* **Systemic Function**: Acts as a liquid buffer to survive short-term funding stress under the Basel III LCR constraint.33  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Value is preserved; hoarded by banks, reducing market velocity.33

### **Collateral Velocity**

* **Definition**: The average number of times a single piece of pledged collateral is reused or repledged across the financial system.23  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Off-balance sheet registers of bank/dealer collateral desks.23  
* **Systemic Function**: Determines the multiplier effect of secured credit in the shadow banking system.23  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Contracts sharply under counterparty risk, leading to credit tightening.39

### **Rehypothecation**

* **Definition**: The regulatory right of a prime broker or dealer to reuse collateral pledged by its clients for its own funding or trading purposes.37  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Off-balance sheet transition asset/liability.24  
* **Systemic Function**: Lubricates wholesale markets and lowers financing costs for prime brokerage clients.37  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Frozen or withdrawn, driving deleveraging.39

### **Eurodollars**

* **Definition**: Unsecured U.S. dollar deposit liabilities booked at banks located outside the United States.20  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Liabilities of Offshore Commercial Banks.29  
* **Systemic Function**: Supports international trade invoicing and global wholesale bank funding.29  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Vulnerable to funding freezes, requiring central bank swap line intervention.9

### **Selected Deposits**

* **Definition**: Onshore U.S. dollar wholesale deposits that are economically similar to Eurodollars but booked at onshore U.S. branches.20  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Liabilities of Onshore U.S. Branches of FBOs.20  
* **Systemic Function**: Replaced Eurodollars as the primary domestic unsecured wholesale funding instrument.20  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Subject to rapid pricing shifts based on Fed reserve conditions.20

### **Covered Interest Parity (CIP)**

* **Definition**: The arbitrage condition stating that the interest rate differential between two currencies should equal the premium/discount on the forward exchange rate.17  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Market Pricing Relationship (No balance sheet location).17  
* **Systemic Function**: Represents the theoretical benchmark for risk-free international capital mobility and FX swap efficiency.17  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Violations widen, indicating severe balance sheet constraints.31

### **Margin Spiral**

* **Definition**: A destabilizing feedback loop where declining speculator capital leads to reduced market liquidity, higher volatility, and increased margin requirements, forcing further deleveraging.40  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Systemic Network Dynamic.40  
* **Systemic Function**: Drives sudden, non-linear collapses in asset prices and market liquidity.40  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Self-reinforcing; requires central bank liquidity support to break the cycle.40

### **Loss Spiral**

* **Definition**: A feedback loop where asset price declines inflict immediate paper losses on leveraged speculators, forcing asset sales that depress prices further.40  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Balance sheets of Speculators/Dealers.40  
* **Systemic Function**: Amplifies the initial wealth shock and drives asset price divergence from fundamental value.40  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Co-moves with the margin spiral to drive systemic liquidity crises.40

### **Time Preference**

* **Definition**: The relative valuation placed on receiving a good at an earlier date compared with receiving it at a later date.13  
* **Balance Sheet Location**: Private Actor Preferences (No balance sheet location).13  
* **Systemic Function**: Establishes the real rate of interest in organic savings models.13  
* **Behavior under Stress**: Shifts toward immediate preference, driving cash-hoarding.13
