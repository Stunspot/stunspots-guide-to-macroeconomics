# **J. Information Liquidity, Narrative Markets, and Attention Allocation Systems — How Financial Conditions Shape Visibility, Media Incentives, and Public-Reality Formation**

## **Domain Orientation: Information, Expectation, and Macroeconomic Reality**

Within the macro-financial framework established in previous reports, the physical movement of capital and the pricing of risk assets are governed by a foundational layer of coordinated expectations.1 While preceding analyses have mapped the mechanics of monetary ontology, liquidity physics, sovereign debt dynamics, and credit intermediation, this report examines the fundamental substrate through which these systems operate: the information environment.3 Information does not merely serve as a neutral, reflective mirror of macroeconomic fundamentals. Instead, in reflexive, complex financial systems, the production, distribution, and consumption of information act as primary transmission channels that determine how capital is allocated, how institutional legitimacy is preserved or eroded, and how systemic crises are triggered and propagated.1

To understand the systemic transmission of economic reality, it is necessary to carry forward the structural distinctions among money, credit, collateral, and liquidity.3 Just as a financial asset requires market liquidity—the ease of converting an asset into cash without causing a significant price impact—so too does a fact, investigation, or structural warning require information liquidity to become an actionable economic belief.6 Where Report I demonstrated the specialized mechanics of venture finance in translating technological expectations into material capital formation, this report generalizes that mechanism across the entire macroeconomy.4

Narratives are not merely decorative commentary sitting on top of the real economy; they are highly structured coordination instruments.9 In a state of radical, Knightian uncertainty, where future outcomes cannot be mapped using standard probability distributions, economic actors rely on narrative frameworks to make decisions.11 These narratives actively shape expectations, guide capital flows, alter discount rates, coordinate behavior, and define what society can publicly perceive.1

The governing proposition of this analysis is that a fact can be mathematically true and yet remain economically illiquid and invisible if it lacks the funding, distribution access, institutional endorsement, reputational safety, or platform fit required to travel through the public attention system.6 Conversely, a structurally weak, empirically flawed narrative can become highly liquid and economically powerful if it is repeated by high-status actors, priced by reflexive markets, and amplified by financially constrained distribution channels.9 Public reality is the socially actionable map that institutions, markets, and elites behave as if true.5 Managing this shared interpretive layer is a primary function of institutional signaling and corporate public relations.4 When a gap opens between slow-moving material reality and the fast-moving public reality, the resulting correction is not merely a repricing of assets, but a structural fracture of public trust and systemic stability.5

## **The Grammar of Narrative Liquidity: Defining the Operational Layer**

To build a reusable framework for diagnostic and predictive analysis, we must operationalize the terms of the information environment. Information liquidity is not identical to content abundance; indeed, an environment characterized by information toxicity and content saturation can suffer from severe information illiquidity.6 A society can be inundated with low-overhead, algorithmically generated content while remaining entirely starved of the high-fidelity, verified facts required to monitor systemic risks.14

Narrative liquidity represents the capacity of a story to circulate, attract repetition, coordinate actors, and attach itself directly to capital, public policy, or group identity.8 It behaves according to epidemiological dynamics, wherein a story spreads through person-to-person and network-to-network transmission, conforming to the classical Kermack-McKendrick Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) model of disease spread.10 Public reality is the operational consensus layer through which economic agents coordinate.5 A bank remains solvent in public reality until a high-velocity rumor coordinates depositors to withdraw their uninsured funds, at which point the bank is liquidated, converting the subjective rumor into a material, lagging fact.13

To evaluate how different information objects succeed or fail in entering public reality, we formulate the Narrative Liquidity Model:

Narrative Liquidity = Funding Capacity x Distribution Access x Institutional Endorsement x Emotional Transmissibility x Reputational Safety x Evidentiary Simplicity x Platform Fit x Market Actionability

Under this model, different categories of information encounter distinct structural constraints:

* **Investigative Stories:** These objects typically fail to achieve narrative liquidity during macroeconomic contractions. Their production requires extensive funding capacity (Funding Capacity), they face high legal and reputational risks (Reputational Safety), and they are structurally complex (Evidentiary Simplicity), rendering them highly illiquid despite their empirical accuracy.6  
* **Complex Macroeconomic Forecasts:** These analyses fail to circulate widely because they lack emotional transmissibility (Emotional Transmissibility), are cognitively complex (Evidentiary Simplicity), and have poor alignment with the engagement-driven algorithms of distribution platforms (Platform Fit), restricting their audience to specialized institutional niches.2  
* **Elite-Threatening Disclosures:** These warnings fail because they cannot secure high-status institutional endorsement (Institutional Endorsement) and carry severe reputational and career-safety penalties (Reputational Safety) for the professional actors who would otherwise distribute them.4  
* **Crisis Bank-Run Rumors:** These achieve hyper-liquidity because they possess extreme emotional transmissibility (Emotional Transmissibility) driven by fear, require no formal funding, have perfect platform compatibility (Platform Fit), and offer immediate market actionability (Market Actionability) as depositors act to preserve their capital.5  
* **Artificial Intelligence and Speculative Technology Narratives:** These achieve massive liquidity because they are supported by abundant venture capital funding (Funding Capacity), receive continuous institutional endorsement from high-status allocators (Institutional Endorsement), offer high speculative optionality, and are simplified into visually compelling future-focused slides (Evidentiary Simplicity).8  
* **Local Corruption Stories:** These remain illiquid because they suffer from weak distribution access (Distribution Access) due to the systemic collapse of regional newsrooms, and they lack a global emotional hook (Emotional Transmissibility).18  
* **Austerity Narratives:** These gain liquidity when creditor institutions and sovereign rating agencies endorse them (Institutional Endorsement), using them to coordinate fiscal policies despite their contractionary economic impacts.  
* **Speculative Bubble Narratives:** These are validated by reflexive price action (Market Actionability); as long as prices rise, they supply apparent empirical evidence (Evidentiary Simplicity) that validates the underlying narrative, creating a self-reinforcing loop.4

## **The Procyclical Attention Engine: How Credit Cycles Dictate Public Perception**

The structural visibility of information in a macroeconomy is governed by a procyclical attention engine tied directly to the credit cycle.14 During expansionary phases characterized by low discount rates and aggressive credit creation, the commercial foundations of the media and corporate communication sectors expand.25 This expansion is driven by the highly procyclical nature of corporate advertising expenditures.25

Historically, the nominal elasticity of advertising spend with respect to GDP was estimated at approximately 1.4; however, econometric studies covering the period following the late 1990s reveal that this elasticity has structurally increased to approximately 1.9.25 This structural break coincides with the rise of digital and internet-based advertising, which has accelerated the sensitivity of corporate ad spend to broader business cycles.26

In an abundant-liquidity regime, rising ad revenues and low financing costs allow media conglomerates to expand newsrooms, fund expensive long-term investigative reporting, establish international bureaus, and tolerate significant legal and reputational risks.14 Concurrently, corporate public-relations budgets, brand sponsorships, and speculative creator campaigns grow, creating an environment that readily rewards optimistic, forward-looking narratives and speculative technology paradigms.10

When the monetary regime tightens, this process reverses. Rising discount rates and contracting corporate margins force managers to execute immediate cuts to discretionary expenditures, with marketing and advertising budgets serving as the primary targets.25 The resulting contraction in advertising revenues triggers immediate, structural newsroom redundancies.14 The scale of this collapse is documented across the media ecosystem:

* In the year 2025 alone, over 17,000 jobs in news, entertainment, streaming, and broadcasting vanished, representing an 18% spike from the already punishing cuts of 2024.28  
* By early 2026, the structural downsizing accelerated: the BBC announced plans to cut up to 2,000 positions, representing approximately one in ten staff members.14  
* CBS News implemented a 6% staff reduction, including the complete shutdown of CBS News Radio.14  
* The Washington Post implemented a massive workforce reduction, eliminating over 300 Guild-covered positions.14 When factoring in non-Guild personnel, managers, editors, and the closure of key foreign infrastructure—such as the entire Seoul news hub and half of the paper's global bureau network—the purge eliminated between 44% and 47.5% of the paper's active journalists, reducing a pre-layoff newsroom of 790 down to a severely depleted core.27  
* The Associated Press implemented voluntary buyouts targeting Guild members to reduce global staff by less than 5% as licensing contracts with declining US print newspapers terminated.14  
* Other major cuts occurred across Politico (3% of staff), the Atlanta Journal-Constitution (15% of staff), Us Weekly (nearly half of its 50-person staff), and the London Standard (reducing staff from 75 to 16).31

The destruction of media operating capital directly impairs the production of public reality.14 Investigative journalism is slow, labor-intensive, structurally uncertain, and legally hazardous.6 It requires months of forensic document analysis, source cultivation, travel, and extensive pre-publication legal review—activities that represent an unsustainable corporate expense when newsrooms face existential budget crises.6

Consequently, contractions in credit and advertising systematically starve investigative reporting.14 To survive, media organizations are forced to substitute capital-intensive investigative reporting with cheap, low-overhead, algorithmically optimized alternatives: content aggregation, programmatic SEO farming, sponsored content, and sensationalized, emotionally polarizing commentary.6 Access journalism—where reporters trade critical scrutiny for continued access to high-status sources—becomes the dominant editorial strategy because it requires minimal investigative budget.6

Thus, a structural information failure occurs: precisely when a tightening cycle begins to expose balance-sheet decay, corporate fraud, and systemic risks, the media institutions responsible for monitoring these risks are least financially capable of doing so.14

## **A Theory of Narrative Markets: Competitive Belief Systems**

A narrative market is a competitive system where stories, frames, and interpretive models compete for scarce societal attention, public trust, capital allocation, repetition, and institutional adoption.4 Narrative markets do not trade in physical commodities or legal claims on cash flows; instead, they trade in the currencies of belief, status, visibility, funding, and regulatory permission.4

The participants in these markets represent a diverse array of public, private, and decentralized actors, each operating with distinct institutional incentives and distribution leverage.6 The competitive dynamics of a narrative market are highly reflexive.1 When an asset price rises, the narrative explaining that price appreciation gains immediate legitimacy and liquidity, coordinating further capital inflows and driving prices higher.10 Conversely, short sellers and activist researchers engage in narrative arbitrage, seeking to identify assets where the dominant public narrative has completely decoupled from slow-moving material fundamentals.7 They publish detailed forensic reports to puncture the prevailing narrative, attempting to trigger a high-velocity repricing and capture profits on short positions.7

## **Attention Allocation Asymmetry: The Structural Mechanics of Visibility**

Not all facts receive equal oxygen in the modern financial system. The distribution of attention is characterized by severe structural asymmetries, systematically prioritizing specific classes of information while leaving others entirely starved of public visibility.7 In an information environment dominated by high-frequency digital platforms, a story’s capacity to capture attention is not determined by its empirical accuracy or its long-term systemic importance.6 Instead, visibility is governed by alignment with the cognitive parameters of the human brain and the algorithmic incentives of distribution networks.9

A complex, highly accurate warning regarding balance-sheet decay or structural leverage is inherently illiquid because it fails the terms of simplicity, platform fit, and audience readiness.2 It requires deep domain knowledge, complex mathematical processing, and sustained cognitive focus—attributes that are severely penalized by engagement-driven programmatic feeds.6

Conversely, a simple, empirically flawed narrative that is emotionally charged, carries low cognitive friction, and serves the immediate financial or political interests of high-status actors can achieve near-instantaneous, global liquidity.9 This structural asymmetry creates a persistent misalignment between public reality and slow-moving material fundamentals, allowing systemic imbalances, corporate frauds, and speculative bubbles to grow unmonitored until a sudden, catastrophic crisis forces a violent convergence.5

## **Media Financing Regimes and Platform Disintermediation**

The operational viability of the public attention system is governed by a series of highly constrained financing models, each carrying structural biases that dictate the boundaries of public reality.6 The historic shift from print-advertising dominance to search and social-media referral dependence has systematically stripped publishers of pricing power.20 This structural vulnerability has entered an acute phase with the rapid, post-2024 deployment of Generative AI Search and conversational "answer engines".20

The integration of Google AI Overviews and AI Mode directly at the top of search result pages has fundamentally broken the traditional web economics.20 A comprehensive Ahrefs study published in February 2026 revealed that the presence of AI Overviews correlates with a **58% reduction in click-through rates (CTR)** to the source websites hosting the original content.21 This represents a near-doubling of the 34.5% decline documented in early 2025.21

For informational and utility queries, SparkToro and Exposure Ninja data show that the "zero-click" rate—where a user obtains an answer directly from the AI summary without clicking any external web link—reaches an astonishing 80% to 83%.32 On the corporate side, publishers represented by Digital Content Next report median organic traffic losses ranging between 10% and 25%, with severe outliers experiencing search referral collapses exceeding 75%.21 In some highly exposed content niches, such as lifestyle, travel, and utility queries, publishers have recorded click drops of up to 89%.20

The systemic response of the publishing industry has been a mixture of legal resistance and existential panic. Major conglomerates, including Penske Media, have filed antitrust lawsuits against search platforms, while the European Publishers Council filed formal complaints with the European Commission.21 These complaints argue that AI search engines present a coercive "forced choice": publishers must permit their proprietary reporting to be extracted for AI training and inline summaries, or opt-out of AI search indexing and face immediate exclusion from Google Search altogether, resulting in digital erasure.21

In response, platforms have introduced minor concessions, such as "further exploration" links, inline link hover cards, and subscription labels.21 Yet, search executives continue to justify the shift, framing the lost referral traffic as merely eliminating low-value "bounce clicks".41

## **Elite Signaling as Institutional Coordination**

In a complex macroeconomic environment characterized by radical, Knightian uncertainty, institutional signaling acts as the primary mechanism to reduce cognitive chaos and establish a coordinated basis for action.4 Elites do not merely communicate facts; they deploy structured signals designed to manage expectations, coordinate subordinate actors, suppress volatility, and occasionally construct a self-protective fog to obscure systemic vulnerabilities.3

Central banks manage the yield curve not only through open-market transactions, but also through forward guidance—speeches, dot plots, and reaction functions that signal monetary intentions.3 Corporate executives employ share buybacks, earnings call language, and strategic layoffs to project stability and preserve valuation multipliers.11

Venture capitalists publish partner memos and coordinate promotional campaigns to direct retail and institutional capital toward speculative sectors.4 When these signals coordinate market expectations successfully, they function as self-fulfilling stabilization mechanisms; when they fail, they reveal a profound loss of institutional legitimacy, accelerating systemic panics.5

## **Reputation-Risk Economics and Corporate Safeguards**

In modern corporate governance, reputation is not an emotional or marketing concept; it is a highly volatile financial asset that directly impacts a firm's cost of capital, customer lifetime value, talent acquisition, and regulatory exposure.12 Empirical research in corporate finance indicates that more than 25% of a publicly traded enterprise's total market value is directly attributable to its intangible brand equity and reputational standing.45

Consequently, the occurrence of unexpected adverse media announcements—such as cybersecurity breaches, compliance failures, operational risk disclosures, or litigation threats—acts as a severe financial shock.22 These announcements reveal critical deficiencies in corporate governance, internal controls, and risk management systems, triggering immediate repricing by equity and debt markets.22

To quantify the economic impact of reputational damage, risk managers utilize the Reputation Risk Cost (RRC) metric 12:

RRC = Change in CAC + Change in SalesCycle + Change in CPH + Change in SalaryPremium + Change in ChurnLoss + Change in CrisisOps

Each factor represents a measurable corporate penalty:

* **Change in CAC** represents the increase in Customer Acquisition Cost as trust-eroded prospects require higher advertising outlays to convert.12  
* **Change in SalesCycle** represents the lengthening of the sales cycle as corporate B2B clients demand extensive compliance and validation before contract signing.12  
* **Change in CPH** represents the increase in Cost Per Hire to attract qualified personnel to a tarnished brand.12  
* **Change in SalaryPremium** represents the "trust premium" salary increase required to overcome negative public perception.12  
* **Change in ChurnLoss** represents the direct revenue lost from increased customer and client churn.12  
* **Change in CrisisOps** represents the operational expenditures of crisis-management PR retainers, forensic audit teams, and legal defense costs.12

Because reputation risk is highly quantifiable, corporations have developed aggressive defensive mechanisms to insulate themselves from negative disclosures. Risk officers deploy automated, continuous adverse-media screening platforms to monitor global news, regulatory announcements, and social-media signals in real-time.46 When potential reputational threats are detected, corporations activate structured, 48-hour crisis-suppression playbooks.12 These playbooks focus on controlling the search engine results page (SERP) for brand-related queries, deploying litigious or uncertainty-laden tones in media statements to neutralize facts, and leveraging public relations networks to drown out critical reporting.12

Concurrently, a major structural development occurred in April 2026, when the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) finalized a new rule prohibiting financial institutions from weaponizing informal "reputation risk" guidelines.48 Historically, regulators and banks had utilized vague reputation risk standards to unilaterally deny credit, terminate merchant accounts, or close banking relationships for politically disfavored but entirely law-abiding businesses.48 The 2026 rule represents a significant effort to decouple formal financial compliance from subjective, narrative-driven reputational judgements, establishing that access to the banking system must be granted based on objective solvency and credit-risk metrics rather than public-relations exposure.48

## **Market-Moving Narratives and Reflexive Asset Pricing**

In public markets, narratives do not merely sit on top of prices; they actively shape capital positioning, which in turn dictates price action.1 When a narrative gains sufficient liquidity, it alters the perceived probability of future outcomes, directly changing how investors calculate expected cash flows and discount rates.2 This process is highly differentiated based on the investor clientele in question.7

Empirical research in behavioral finance reveals a striking divergence between the market-pricing impact of retail investor attention versus institutional investor attention 7:

* **Aggregate Retail Attention (ARA):** Surges in retail attention to specific stocks negatively predict future market-wide returns on a one-week-ahead basis.7 Retail attention is highly procyclical, driven by emotional transmissibility, high-status creator hype, and simple narrative structures.9 This retail-driven attention coordinates aggregate buying imbalances, creating temporary, emotional price pressure that drives stocks significantly above their fundamental value.7 This retail overvaluation is transitory and systematically reverses over subsequent weeks, particularly during periods of high macroeconomic uncertainty and poor market-wide liquidity.7  
* **Aggregate Institutional Attention (AIA):** In contrast, institutional attention—when measured prior to major corporate or macroeconomic announcements—positively predicts subsequent market returns.7 Institutional attention is highly analytical, focused on resolving structural uncertainty, pricing systemic risk, and capturing fundamental risk premiums.2 When institutions coordinate attention on a specific theme, it leads to more efficient price discovery and the long-term accrual of risk premiums rather than short-term, speculative overvaluation.7

## **Narrative-Finance Coupling and Speculative Capital**

The relationship between capital flows and narrative liquidity is characterized by deep reflexivity, generating self-reinforcing loops that drive asset valuations far beyond fundamental anchors.1 In narrative-finance coupling, a narrative does not merely explain a price change; the price change itself acts as the primary validation of the narrative's truth.1 This reflexive coupling progresses through a highly structured lifecycle, moving from speculative emergence to catastrophic correction.10

The dynamic mechanics of this cycle conform to the classic Kermack-McKendrick SIR (Susceptible, Infected, Recovered) epidemic model adapted for economic contagion.10 In the initial phase, a massive pool of market participants is "susceptible" to a new narrative of growth or technological disruption.10 The introduction of a charismatic founder, a compelling PowerPoint slide, or an initial capital investment acts as the "infection" event.17 As capital begins to flow toward the narrative, asset prices rise. This price appreciation is widely covered by financially constrained media outlets eager for high-traffic stories, transforming the narrative into a consensus belief and rapidly increasing the "infected" population.10

In loose-liquidity regimes, this loop is supercharged. Abundant capital gives narratives physical, material bodies: startups hire thousands of employees, purchase billboards, fund massive PR campaigns, sponsor academic research, and build political constituencies.4 Skepticism becomes extremely costly, both in terms of missing out on investment returns and in terms of reputational damage within elite networks.6

However, this coupling contains the seeds of its own destruction. As the narrative progresses, the pool of "susceptible" investors is fully exhausted, and the market becomes completely "infected".10 At this point, the asset is priced for perfection, requiring continuous, massive inflows of capital to sustain its valuation.10 The transition to a "recovered" (or forgetting) phase is triggered when a liquidity shock or an undeniable fundamental gap emerges—such as a failure to generate cash flows or the collapse of a key firm.10 The loop then reverses violently: price declines discredit the narrative, triggering panic, capital flight, and narrative collapse.10

## **Crisis Narrative Formation and High-Velocity Panics**

During a macroeconomic or systemic banking crisis, the timeline of information transmission is compressed to near-zero, transforming the mechanics of financial panic.5 Crises represent periods of extreme, acute information asymmetry where established public reality fractures, forcing market participants to make rapid decisions to preserve capital.3 The first plausible interpretive frame that gains widespread distribution acts as a powerful cognitive anchor, dictating how the entire event is interpreted and whether panic will accelerate.16

The ultimate expression of this dynamic is the first social-media-accelerated bank run, exemplified by the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) on March 10, 2023.5 Traditional bank runs were physically and technologically constrained: depositors communicated via slow channels like mail, telephone, or physical queues, allowing regulators and bank managers time to intervene, halt trading, or provide official reassurance.16 In the digital era, however, social media platforms have compressed this timeline to near-zero, creating an unprecedented channel of systemic run risk.5

Empirical research on the SVB run demonstrates that pre-existing exposure to highly networked social media communities acted as a massive risk amplifier 5:

* **Networked Depositor Clusters:** SVB's depositor base was highly concentrated within the close-knit venture capital and technology founder community.5 This community exhibited an exceptionally high degree of communication on Twitter/X, Discord, and private founder chat groups.5  
* **The Velocity of Panic:** When SVB announced a balance-sheet restructuring and unrealized bond losses, this highly networked depositor cluster immediately coordinated on social media.5 Depositors posted 6,528 "run-related" tweets containing explicit flight keywords (such as "withdraw") between March 8 and March 13, 2023—roughly five times the volume of the next most discussed bank, First Republic.13  
* **Social Amplification of Balance-Sheet Risk:** Econometric analysis reveals that banks in the top third of social media exposure lost significantly more market value and experienced far larger deposit outflows during the regional banking crisis.37 This effect remains highly significant even after controlling for fundamental balance-sheet risk factors, such as mark-to-market bond losses and the ratio of uninsured deposits.13

Social media does not merely reflect bank distress; it actively amplifies it by allowing depositors to coordinate withdrawal behaviors in real-time.5 When depositors observe prominent venture capitalists publicly advising companies to withdraw funds, they recognize that other depositors will run, making immediate flight the only rational, self-preserving choice.16 The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) estimated that customers withdrew $40 billion—one-fifth of SVB’s entire deposit base—in a matter of hours, forcing regulators to shut down the bank before midday rather than waiting for the close of business, exposing a critical regulatory inability to keep pace with digital coordination.16
